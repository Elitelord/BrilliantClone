## 1. CED Framework & Unit Placement

### DOK 1 — Facts

- AP Human Geography Unit 2 is **Population and Migration Patterns and Processes** [S1].
- Topic **2.5** is **The Demographic Transition Model** [S1].
- Learning Objective **IMP-2.B**: *Explain theories of population growth and decline* [S1].
- Essential Knowledge **IMP-2.B.1**: *The demographic transition model can be used to explain population change over time* [S1].
- Essential Knowledge **IMP-2.B.2**: *The epidemiological transition explains causes of changing death rates* [S1].
- Enduring Understanding **IMP-2**: *Changes in population are due to mortality, fertility, and migration, which are influenced by the interplay of environmental, economic, cultural, and political factors* [S1].
- Topic **2.3** covers **Population Composition**; population pyramids are explicitly tied to composition, growth prediction, and markets [S1, S2].
- Topic **2.4** covers population dynamics (growth/decline trends, momentum) [S1, S8].
- Topic **2.9** (aging populations) links to **dependency ratio** [S2, S8].
- Economic sectors are formally in **Unit 7** (Topic 7.2), but are routinely **cross-linked back to Unit 2 DTM** in instruction [S3, S4].
- College Board sample activity for 2.5: students pair terms (CBR, CDR, NIR, total population), place pyramids at DTM stages, and justify choices [S1].
- College Board sample activity for 2.3: students **draw** population pyramids for subnational scenarios and explain thinking [S1].
- Macmillan AP modules place DTM in **Module 10**, after distribution, composition, and dynamics modules [S5].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**Where these concepts live in the official curriculum:** The CED treats DTM and ETM as paired **theories of population change** under IMP-2.B, while population pyramids sit under **composition** (2.3) as visual tools that *predict* growth and policy needs. Sectors are officially Unit 7 content but function as a **third explanatory lens** on development in nearly every syllabus. Standard instruction therefore spirals: define rates → model stages → explain *why* death rates fall (ETM) → read pyramids → connect to sectors/dependency → apply to real countries → critique the model. The exam expects **cross-topic synthesis**, not isolated memorization of five stage names.

## 2. Core Vocabulary & Formulas

### DOK 1 — Facts

- **Crude Birth Rate (CBR):** live births per 1,000 people per year [S6, S7].
- **Crude Death Rate (CDR):** deaths per 1,000 people per year [S6, S7].
- **Rate of Natural Increase (RNI / NIR):** CBR − CDR (often per 1,000); can be expressed as a **percentage** by dividing by 10 (e.g., gap of 20 → 2.0% NIR) [S6, S7, app].
- **Total Fertility Rate (TFR):** average number of children a woman will have in her lifetime [S6, S7].
- **Replacement-level fertility:** typically ~2.1 children per woman (accounts for child mortality) [S6, S7].
- **Infant Mortality Rate (IMR):** deaths of infants under age 1 per 1,000 live births [S6].
- **Life expectancy:** average years a person is expected to live [S6, S7].
- **Doubling time (Rule of 70):** approximate years until population doubles = **70 ÷ growth rate (%)** [S8, S9].
- **Population momentum / demographic momentum:** population keeps growing after fertility hits replacement because large young cohorts are entering childbearing years [S8].
- **Dependency ratio:** (population aged 0–14 + population aged 65+) ÷ working-age population (15–64), often × 100 [S8, S10].
- **Youth dependency:** dependents under 15 relative to working-age [S8].
- **Elderly dependency:** dependents 65+ relative to working-age [S8].
- **Cohort:** group sharing a temporal experience (e.g., 5-year birth cohort in pyramids) [S11].
- **Expansive pyramid shape:** wide base, high growth [S11, S12].
- **Stationary pyramid shape:** base similar width to other cohorts, slow/no growth [S11, S12].
- **Constrictive pyramid shape:** narrow base, decline [S11, S12].
- **Primary sector:** extraction (agriculture, mining, fishing, logging) [S3, S4].
- **Secondary sector:** manufacturing and construction [S3, S4].
- **Tertiary sector:** services (retail, healthcare, education, transport) [S3, S4].
- **Quaternary sector:** information, research, knowledge processing [S3].
- **Quinary sector:** top-level decision-making (executives, government leaders) [S3].
- **Clark-Fisher model:** economies shift employment from primary → secondary → tertiary (and beyond) as development proceeds [S3, S4].
- **Demographic dividend:** economic growth potential when working-age share rises relative to dependents [S7].
- **Carrying capacity:** maximum population an environment can support [S1].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**The vocabulary layer:** AP instruction builds a **shared measurement toolkit** before or alongside the DTM story. Students must fluently move between **absolute rates** (CBR/CDR per 1,000), **derived indicators** (NIR, TFR, IMR), and **structural visuals** (pyramids). Formulas are not abstract math drills — they are tools for comparing countries and justifying stage assignments. Doubling time and momentum explain why Stage 3 countries still grow after births fall; dependency ratio explains why Stage 4/5 create pension and healthcare pressure. Sector vocabulary from Unit 7 is folded into Unit 2 so students can say *why* birth rates fall (urbanization, female education, off-farm employment) rather than only *that* they fall.

## 3. Demographic Transition Model (DTM)

### DOK 1 — Facts

- DTM describes how **birth rates, death rates, and population growth** change as societies move from pre-industrial to post-industrial conditions [S6, S7].
- Warren Thompson developed the framework in **1929** from **European** demographic history [S7].
- Original model had **four stages**; **Stage 5** (decline) is widely taught but **debated** on exams [S6, S7, S13].
- Stage names used in AP materials:
  - Stage 1: **High Stationary**
  - Stage 2: **Early Expanding**
  - Stage 3: **Late Expanding**
  - Stage 4: **Low Stationary**
  - Stage 5: **Declining** [S6, S7, app]
- **Stage 1:** high CBR (~35–40+), high CDR (~35–40+), very low NIR, minimal growth [S6, S7].
- **Stage 2:** CBR stays high (~35–45); CDR falls sharply (~15–20); **very high NIR**; "population explosion" / J-curve growth [S6, S7, S8].
- Stage 2 drivers of falling death rates: improved sanitation, clean water, medicine, vaccinations, better nutrition [S6, S7].
- Stage 2 birth rates remain high due to **cultural lag** — norms haven't caught up to new survival realities [S7].
- **Stage 3:** CBR declines (~20–25); CDR low (~8–12); NIR moderates; growth slows [S6, S7].
- Stage 3 birth decline drivers: urbanization, female education, contraception access, shift away from agrarian labor needs [S6, S7].
- **Stage 4:** low CBR (~10–15); low CDR (~8–12); NIR near zero; stable/slow growth [S6, S7].
- **Stage 5:** CBR falls **below** CDR; negative NIR; population decline; aging society [S6, S7, app].
- Two **stable** configurations: Stage 1 (high-high) and Stage 4 (low-low) — both near-zero NIR [app, S7].
- Death rates typically fall **before** birth rates in the transition [S6, S7, app].
- DTM is a **generalized model** — real countries deviate in timing, speed, and path [S6, S13].
- Example countries commonly cited:
  - Stage 1 (rare today): remote traditional societies [S7]
  - Stage 2: **Nigeria**, Niger, many Sub-Saharan nations [S6, S7, app]
  - Stage 3: **Brazil**, India (transitional) [S6, app]
  - Stage 4: **United States**, Western Europe [S6, app]
  - Stage 5: **Japan**, Italy, Germany [S6, S7, app]
- **England** is the classic historical case for entering Stage 2 around **1800** (Industrial Revolution) [app, S7].
- Fiveable notes **grandparents emerge** as a visible 3-generation phenomenon in Stage 2 when life expectancy rises [S14].
- CED sample MC: moving Stage 2→3 characterized by **increasing female education** (not declining death rate) [S1].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**The DTM narrative consensus:** Textbooks and review sites converge on a **single causal story**: development improves survival first (death rates drop), which widens the birth-death gap and triggers rapid growth; only later do social and economic changes pull fertility down. The model is taught as a **temporal graph** (rates vs. time/stage) and as a **classification scheme** for countries. Instructors stress that Stage 2 is the demographic "engine" of the modern population boom, Stage 3 is the "slowing but still growing" phase (momentum), and Stages 4–5 flip the policy problem from youth bulges to aging. The app's Lesson 1 (`dtm-engine`) mirrors this consensus closely: CBR/CDR → real countries → NIR formula → five-stage idealized graph → predict/solve/connect → curve-drawing synthesis.

## 4. Epidemiological Transition Model (ETM)

### DOK 1 — Facts

- ETM explains **why death rates change** — the mortality-side companion to DTM [S1, S15].
- Abdel **Omran** proposed the model in **1971** ("The Epidemiologic Transition") [S15, S16].
- ETM tracks shifts in **dominant causes of death** as societies develop [S15, S16].
- ETM stages (Omran / AP framing):
  - **Age of Pestilence and Famine** (aligns DTM Stage 1): infectious disease, famine, high mortality all ages [S15].
  - **Age of Receding Pandemics** (DTM Stage 2): sanitation, medicine reduce infectious deaths [S15].
  - **Age of Degenerative and Chronic Diseases** (Stages 3–4): heart disease, cancer, stroke dominate; affect older ages [S15, S7].
  - **Age of Delayed Degenerative Diseases** (Stage 4+): chronic disease still leads but onset delayed by lifestyle/medicine [S15].
- Some materials add **re-emerging infectious disease** (antibiotic resistance, COVID-19) as a Stage 5 echo [S15].
- Omran described transition **models**: classical, accelerated, delayed [S16].
- McKinlay & McKinlay (1977) challenged whether **medical measures** alone explain US mortality decline [S16].
- Wilkinson (1994): shift from **material scarcity** to **social disadvantage** as health driver [S16].
- ETM directly explains the **sharp CDR drop in DTM Stage 2** [S7, S15].
- Standard classroom pairing: **Day 1 = DTM lecture; Day 2 = ETM lecture** plus LDC vs MDC country comparison [S17].
- CED ties ETM to IMP-2.B.2 explicitly — students must connect death-rate mechanics to DTM stages [S1].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**Why ETM is taught with DTM:** The DTM shows *what* happens to rates; ETM explains *why deaths move*. Consensus pedagogy pairs them so students don't treat falling CDR as magic — they link it to sanitation, germ theory, vaccines, then later to lifestyle diseases in aging populations. This pairing also sets up pyramid reading (thin tops in Stage 1 = short lives; full tops in Stage 4/5 = longevity). **Gap vs BrilliantClone:** ETM is listed in PRD as post-MVP (`Limits of the Model` / epidemiological transition lessons) and is **not yet in the shipped app**, though Lesson 1's lifespan/pyramid content partially gestures at mortality without Omran's framework.

## 5. Population Pyramids

### DOK 1 — Facts

- Population pyramid = horizontal bar chart of **age-sex distribution** [S11, S12].
- Males typically on **left**, females on **right** [S11, app].
- Cohorts usually grouped in **5-year** age bands [S11].
- Three shape categories:
  - **Expansive** — wide base (high growth)
  - **Stationary** — roughly equal cohort widths
  - **Constrictive** — narrow base (decline) [S11, S12]
- Pyramid ↔ DTM stage mapping (consensus):
  - Stage 1: wide base, **steep drop** to top (high births, high deaths, short lives) [app, S12]
  - Stage 2: **broad triangle**, very wide base (high births, falling deaths, young population) [app, S12]
  - Stage 3: **narrowing triangle**, base still wider than top but less extreme [app, S12]
  - Stage 4: **column / rectangular** — cohorts roughly equal (low-low rates) [app, S12]
  - Stage 5: **inverted / top-heavy** — base narrower than middle/elderly (more old than young) [app, S12]
- Wide base signals **high birth rate** and often **positive NIR** [app, S12].
- Thin top in early stages = **high CDR** and short life expectancy [app, S11].
- Fuller top in later stages = **lower CDR** and longer life expectancy [app].
- Pyramids can reveal **historical events**: baby boom (US), one-child policy (China), war losses (male deficits in fighting-age bands) [S12, S18].
- PSO-2.F.1: pyramids used to assess growth/decline and **predict markets** for goods/services [S1].
- Population Education "Power of the Pyramids" activity (grades 9–12):
  - Construct pyramids from country data
  - Correlate shape to growth pattern
  - Infer past events and future trends [S18]
- geteach.com dashboard activity: students fill **19 countries'** indicators, examine pyramids, assign **both DTM and ETM** stages — no single DTM map layer; analytical synthesis required [S9].
- Social Studies Hub best practice: assign **different country pyramids per table group**; groups identify DTM stage, predict challenges, write demographic "headline" [S19].
- TPT pyramid packet: connects pyramids to dependency ratios, migration, policy, aging, gender, conflict — **23 questions**, 50 country examples [S20].
- NVCC textbook: pyramids show composition vs geographic distribution [S11].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**How pyramids are taught:** Consensus moves students from **anatomy** (what bars mean) → **shape vocabulary** (expansive/stationary/constrictive) → **stage classification** → **historical inference** → **forward prediction** (momentum, dependency, policy). The pyramid is not a standalone graphic — it is the bridge between abstract rates and real national stories. Active methods dominate: **draw**, **reshape**, **compare countries**, **justify with evidence**. The app's Lesson 2 (`population-pyramids`) aligns strongly: setup → explore shape with stage picker → lifespan concept → rate sliders linking base width to CBR → MC stage identification → reshape solves → classify Niger/Brazil/Japan/USA. **Relative gap:** classroom materials more often stress **baby boom / policy scarring** and **dependency/migration** links; the app focuses on DTM stage classification over historical scar interpretation.

## 6. Economic Sectors & Development

### DOK 1 — Facts

- Five sectors: primary, secondary, tertiary, quaternary, quinary [S3, S4].
- **Less developed countries (LDCs):** primary + secondary dominate employment [S4].
- **More developed countries (MDCs):** tertiary + quaternary + quinary dominate [S4].
- Primary employment highest in **Stage 1–2** DTM societies [S3, S4].
- Secondary (industry) peaks in **middle development** — industrializing Stage 2–3 [S3, app].
- Tertiary dominates **Stage 4–5** post-industrial economies [S3, S4, app].
- Clark-Fisher model stages:
  - Pre-industrial: primary dominant
  - Industrialization: secondary grows; workers leave farms for factories
  - Post-industrial: tertiary (services) largest [S3, S4].
- Mechanization reduces farm labor needs → pushes workers to cities and factories [S3].
- As incomes rise, demand for **services** increases [S3].
- Informal tertiary sector (street vending, unregistered work) common in developing economies — supports livelihoods but can slow formal sector transition [S4].
- Sector transition speed depends on: trade access, energy/resources, education investment, government stability, FDI [S4].
- CED SPS-7.C.1: **sectoral structure** (formal + informal) is a measure of development alongside GDP, GNI, fertility, IMR, literacy [S1].
- CED links sectoral change to **outsourcing**, SEZs, deindustrialization in Unit 7 [S1].
- Example: US — services ~80%+ of employment; manufacturing share fell over decades [S3].
- Example: South Korea, Taiwan, China (1980s–2000s) — rapid secondary growth [S3].
- Albert.io Clark-Fisher table maps sector dominance to development eras with country examples [S3].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**Sectors as the economic mirror of DTM:** Standard instruction presents sector shift as the **workforce story** behind demographic change: when fewer workers must farm, families urbanize, women enter wage labor, and fertility norms change — linking back to Stage 3 birth decline. The Clark-Fisher model gives a **second curve** (employment shares over time) that parallels the birth/death curves. AP materials treat secondary peak as **mid-transition** (not early Stage 2), which matches the app's feedback ("industry peaks in the MIDDLE of development"). **Unit placement tension:** CED puts sectors in Unit 7, but Rubenstein/Fouberg syllabi and review sites cross-reference them in Unit 2 population discussions. The app's Lesson 3 (`sector-shift`) is therefore **canon-aligned content** even though it lives in a DTM course — it implements the interdisciplinary synthesis the exam expects. **Gap:** app teaches primary/secondary/tertiary only; quaternary/quinary and informal sector are not covered.

## 7. Related Population Concepts

### DOK 1 — Facts

- **Population explosion** begins in DTM Stage 2 when death rates fall before birth rates [S8].
- **J-curve** illustrates exponential world population growth since ~1750 [S8].
- Momentum example: TFR hits replacement but population still grows because **youth bulge** enters reproductive years [S8].
- Fiveable: classic MC stem — fertility at replacement but population still grows → answer references **youthful age structure** [S8].
- Youth dependency high in Stage 2–3 → pressure on education, childcare [S8].
- Elderly dependency rises in Stage 4–5 → pressure on pensions, healthcare, elder care labor [S8, S10].
- Aging driven by: falling fertility, rising life expectancy, lower mortality at older ages [S10].
- PRB / Census data activities: students pull IMR, TFR, GNI, urban %, contraceptive use, compute dependency, net migration [S1, S18].
- **Net migration** affects population change beyond natural increase — DTM alone uses only births/deaths [S7, S13].
- **Malthusian theory** (IMP-2.B.3) — separate LO paired with DTM in CED Topic 2.6 [S1].
- **Women and demographic change** (Topics 2.13–2.14): education, employment, contraception reduce fertility [S1].
- **Population policies** (Topic 2.12): pro-natalist vs anti-natalist; China's one-child policy as exam example [S1, S13].
- HIV/AIDS impacts pyramid shape and dependency in affected countries [S21].
- **Doubling time examples** (Population Education): 0.5% RNI → ~140 years; 1% → ~70 years; 2% → ~35 years [S18].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**The "surrounding ecosystem" of DTM instruction:** A full Unit 2 treatment does not stop at stage labels. Students learn that **structure** (pyramids, dependency) explains **lag effects** (momentum) and **policy problems** (schools vs pensions). Migration, government policy, and gender roles are taught as **forces that bend or bypass** the model. Data literacy — pulling IMR/TFR from PRB, calculating doubling time, filling comparison tables — is a standard scaffold before FRQ-level synthesis. The app embeds doubling time in `dtm.ts` and NIR math in Lesson 1, but **dependency ratio, momentum, migration, Malthus, and population policy** are largely absent from shipped lessons (some flagged post-MVP in PRD).

## 8. Standard Teaching Sequence & Classroom Methods

### DOK 1 — Facts

- CED-recommended progression within Unit 2: distribution → composition → dynamics → **DTM** → Malthus → policies → women → aging → migration [S1, S5].
- Typical **DTM + ETM block:** 2×90-minute classes or 4×45-minute periods [S17].
- Day 1 DTM: bellringer, lecture, notes, quiz [S17].
- Day 2 ETM: lecture, notes, **research assignment comparing one LDC and one MDC** [S17].
- Population Education pyramid lesson usable in math, social studies, science; AP HUG Unit 2 and APES Unit 3 [S18].
- geteach activity: fill **one indicator at a time across all countries** (not one country at a time) to spot regional patterns [S9].
- Social Studies Hub strategies:
  - **Predict before video** (cognitive dissonance)
  - **Pause and discuss** at key moments
  - **Connect across units** (6 and 7 callbacks)
  - **Vocabulary in context**, not front-loaded lists [S19].
- Jigsaw: assign different countries (positive vs negative growth) for demographic indicator research [S1].
- "Making Connections" CED activity: pair CBR/CDR/NIR terms, then place pyramids on DTM stages with justification [S1].
- Group pyramid analysis: describe shape, identify stage, predict challenge, compare groups [S19].
- Dashboard / data sheet approach builds **analytical synthesis** — DTM stage from indicators + pyramid jointly [S9].
- Albert.io FRQ practice: explain India's development level contributing to environmental challenges in Delhi [S1].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**Pedagogical consensus — "how" not just "what":** Standard AP classrooms blend **direct instruction** (lecture + notes on stage definitions) with **data-heavy active learning** (country comparisons, pyramid construction, indicator tables). The dominant pattern is **inductive reasoning from evidence**: students look at real data or visuals first, classify, then receive formal vocabulary. Prediction → manipulation → justification is repeatedly recommended. Cross-unit threading (population → urbanization → development) is explicit in teacher guides. **Contrast with BrilliantClone:** the app **flips the Brilliant playbook** — explore/manipulate first, formalize later — which aligns with Social Studies Hub's "predict first" and Population Education's construct-and-interpret activities, but **de-emphasizes** lecture, jigsaw research, and multi-country spreadsheet work in favor of **guided micro-lessons** with instant feedback.

## 9. Textbook & Syllabus Conventions

### DOK 1 — Facts

- **Rubenstein** (The Cultural Landscape): Ch. 2 Population pp. 44–77; Ch. 3 Migration [S21].
- Rubenstein Ch. 2 key issues: Where distributed? Why increasing? Health challenges? Future growth? [S21].
- **Fouberg** (Human Geography): Ch. 2 Population pp. 36–75; Ch. 3 Migration [S21].
- Fouberg key questions: why uneven distribution? fertility/mortality patterns? migration causes? [S21].
- Both textbooks place DTM inside **population growth and decline over time** section [S21].
- Syllabus Development Guide sample: PRB data table across regions for IMR, TFR, GNI, urban %, contraceptive use [S22].
- Recommended data sources: PRB, US Census, Worldmapper, ArcGIS GeoInquiries [S22].
- Unit 2 in most high school syllabi: **2–4 weeks** [S21, S23].
- Maswood/Richland2 sequence: Unit 2 = Population & Migration (Rubenstein Ch 2–3, Fouberg Ch 2–3) [S23].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**Textbook architecture:** Major AP texts treat DTM as one **pillar** within a broader population chapter that also covers distribution, health, and future growth — not a standalone mini-unit. Migration is **adjacent** (next chapter), but exam FRQs often **recombine** population structure, development, and environmental sustainability (e.g., India/Delhi air quality). Teachers map CED topics onto textbook chapters explicitly in syllabi. The app's **single-course DTM focus** is narrower than a textbook chapter but **deeper** on three interactive lenses than a typical 45-minute class period allows.

## 10. AP Exam Expectations

### DOK 1 — Facts

- DTM appears in **multiple-choice**, **FRQs**, and **stimulus-based** questions (pyramids, vital statistics tables) [S7].
- Students must **recognize all 5 stages** from pyramids and rate data [S14].
- Students must **apply** DTM to specific countries and explain *why* [S7].
- Sample MC (CED): Stage 2→3 transition linked to **female education** [S1].
- Sample MC (CED): characteristic of Stage 2→3 = **high female education** among choices [S1].
- FRQ themes: population pyramids + development + sustainability combined [S1].
- Albert.io warns: **Stage 5 is tested** but less common than Stages 1–4 [S7].
- Common error: assuming **economic development alone** drives fertility — must mention policy, religion, education [S7].
- CountryReports FRQ scaffold: (A) original stage count, (B) two non-Western differences, (C) planning implication [S13].
- Fiveable: connect age structure → future growth in one sentence for momentum credit [S8].
- Economic sector questions appear in Unit 7 but may reference development level from Unit 2 [S3].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**What "knowing DTM" means on the exam:** College Board rewards **multi-factor explanation** and **evidence-based classification**, not stage-name recall. A typical proficient response names the stage, cites **two indicators** (rates, pyramid shape, sector mix, dependency), explains **causal mechanisms**, and acknowledges **limitations or context** (policy, migration). The app's validator-driven interactions train **classification + misconception-specific feedback**, which maps well to MC-style reasoning; less exposure to **multi-stimulus FRQ writing** and **Eurocentric critique** prompts unless post-MVP content ships.

## 11. DTM Limitations & Critiques

### DOK 1 — Facts

- DTM built from **18th–19th century Western Europe** experience [S7, S13].
- Critique: **Eurocentric** — assumes universal linear path [S13].
- Critique: **ignores migration** — immigration swells Stage 4/5 populations; emigration drains Stage 2 [S7, S13].
- Critique: **ignores government policy** — China's one-child policy, European pro-natalist policies [S7, S13].
- Critique: **doesn't predict timing** — only direction of change [S7].
- Critique: **deterministic** — cultural/religious factors can stall or compress transitions [S13].
- **South Korea:** compressed transition Stage 2→5 in ~50 years — faster than Europe [S13].
- **Sub-Saharan Africa:** prolonged Stage 2 beyond model predictions [S13].
- **Saudi Arabia:** high TFR despite high GDP — religion/policy factors [S13].
- Globalization: countries may **skip steps** or transition differently [S13].
- China: policy-driven fertility decline before full industrialization [S13].
- Pandemics, conflict, economic crises can **disrupt** expected patterns [S13].
- PRD (app): post-MVP lesson planned for **Limits of the Model** as standard AP target [app-prd].

### DOK 2 — Summary

**Teaching the critique:** Consensus is not to discard the DTM but to teach it as a **heuristic** — powerful for comparison, weak as prophecy. Critique units often use **paired case studies** (Europe vs South Korea vs Nigeria) and FRQ-style prompts requiring planning implications (infrastructure, pensions, schools). This is **exam-relevant content** increasingly emphasized in review materials. **App gap:** critique/limitations lesson is explicitly **post-MVP**; current lessons present the five-stage model confidently without a structured "where it breaks" beat.

### DOK 2 — Summary

**Alignment snapshot:** The app **over-indexes on consensus strengths** — manipulable visuals, rate-gap intuition, pyramid-DTM mapping, sector-stage linking, real countries, scaffolded difficulty — and matches the **Brilliant + Population Education** learn-by-doing philosophy more than the **lecture + jigsaw spreadsheet** classroom default. It **under-indexes on CED breadth** — ETM, critique, migration, dependency, momentum (explicit), policy, Malthus — and on **historical inference** from pyramid anomalies. Pedagogically it is a **high-quality micro-course** on the DTM *core mechanism* rather than a full Unit 2 replacement. The planned post-MVP lessons (epidemiological transition, limits, place-the-country map) would close the largest CED gaps.
